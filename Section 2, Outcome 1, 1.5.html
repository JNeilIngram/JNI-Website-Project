1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites


When considering the law concerning websites first you need establish which is the governing jurisdiction depending on whether:-

  1. You are the viewer of the content
  2. You are the creator of the content
  3. You are controller of the website
  4. You are the hoster of the website

Each of the above can be in separate countries and/or legal jurisdictions, each with their own legal requirements. Eg. what is considered constitutionally protected free speech in one country may be seen as actionable libel in another or a criminal offence in a third.

The best advice when dealing with contentious issues on a website is simple: If you would not say it in public or to someone's face do not put it online!

Copyright and other interlectual property is also a significant concern. Using images, audio files and video clips without the rights holders consent can potentially lead to (in th UK) a civil action for copyright infringement. This may also lead to further action (eg. defamation or "passing off") depending on the primary content of the website or the context in which the material is used. While some rights holders can take a liberal view with minor infringements (particularly if the context is positive overall and/or the bringing of an action may lead to adverse publicity), some are notorious for their aggressive defence of their intellectual property, notably (in the UK) The Performing Rights Society (covering the vast majority of recording artists) and The Disney Corporation. The only advice that can be given is to always have the rights holders written permission before using any protected intellectual property, and to only use that inellectual property in accordance with the permission given.
