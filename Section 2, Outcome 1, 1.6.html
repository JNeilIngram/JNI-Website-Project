1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them


Disability access when using webpages can be broken down into three core problems:-

  1. navigating the page,
  2. viewing the page,
  3. hearing any audio content associated with a page

When considering the page navigation the designer needs to consider that not all users will be using conventional access devices (eg keyboard or touch screen) if they suffer from physical disabilites. Designers should consider making buttons and links as large as possible without degrading the overall design of the site.

Viewig a page for someone with impaired vision is in itself a multifaceted problem covering different conditions, some of which are:-

  1. Total blindness
  2. Partial blindness
  3. Colour blindness
  4. Epilepsy

Each has its own challenges for the perion affected.

People affected with total blindness tend to use software that "reads" the page to them. Keeping the design of the core parts of a webpage simple should help with this.

Partially sighted people use a mix of the aforesaid readers and/or software/devices that greatly magnify parts of a page at a time. The design of a webpage should reflect this.

Colour blindness is an unusual problem as there are different kinds of colour blindness, but the ability of a webpage to be displayed in monochome and still make sense solves this problem.

Finally epilepsy can be triggered by flashing images, care should be taken avoid these unless necessary, and even then to give a timely warning so that a sufferer can make an informed choice whether or not to view such content.

Those people who partially or totally deaf can be catered for by the provision of subtitles or audio description for video or audio content on a website.
